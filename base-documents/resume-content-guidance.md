For the roles you’re targeting — **Solutions Engineer / Pre-Sales, Implementation Architect, and AI Governance / Risk** — the most effective resume style is **moderately concise but high-signal**. The key is giving **just enough detail to demonstrate credibility and recency**, while avoiding the “overqualified / too senior / too expensive” filter that can appear with very long resumes.

Below is a practical guideline.

---

# Recommended Resume Length & Detail (for your situation)

### Overall length

**2 pages total** is ideal.

Why:

* 1 page can undersell **technical depth and customer-facing experience**
* 3+ pages triggers **“too senior / career historian” bias**
* Recruiters typically spend **20–40 seconds scanning initially**

Your goal is **fast pattern recognition**.

---

# How much detail per role

### Most recent roles (last ~10–12 years)

**5–7 bullet points per role**

Include:

* **What the role was**
* **What problems you solved**
* **What technologies or domains were involved**
* **Customer / enterprise context**
* **Outcome or impact**

Example structure:

```
Senior Solutions Engineer – AI & Analytics
Company | Years

• Led technical discovery and solution architecture for enterprise AI and analytics platforms supporting sales cycles with Fortune 500 customers.
• Designed reference architectures spanning data pipelines, vector search, LLM orchestration, and evaluation frameworks.
• Delivered customer demos, POCs, and architecture workshops translating business problems into deployable solutions.
• Partnered with product and engineering to close technical gaps discovered during pre-sales engagements.
• Advised customers on model evaluation, risk controls, and governance practices for production AI deployments.
```

Notice:

* **technical credibility**
* **customer interaction**
* **architecture thinking**
* **AI governance angle**

All relevant to your target roles.

---

### Mid-career roles (12–20 years ago)

**2–4 bullet points per role**

Purpose:

* Show **experience trajectory**
* Avoid overwhelming the reader with outdated details.

Example:

```
Analytics Architect
Company | Years

• Designed enterprise analytics platforms supporting reporting, forecasting, and operational insights.
• Led cross-functional delivery teams implementing data integration and warehouse solutions.
• Worked directly with business stakeholders to translate analytical requirements into scalable systems.
```

---

### Early career roles (20+ years ago)

Just list the role with **1 bullet or none**.

Example:

```
Data Analyst / BI Developer
Company | Years
• Built reporting and analytics solutions using enterprise BI tools.
```

Or even just:

```
Data Analyst – Company | Years
```

The goal is **credibility without age signaling**.

---

# What matters more than length: signal density

Every bullet should answer at least **one recruiter question**.

For your target roles, those questions are usually:

### Pre-Sales / Solutions Engineer

* Can this person **talk to customers?**
* Can they **design credible architectures?**
* Can they **explain AI/analytics clearly?**

### Implementation / Customer Architect

* Can they **make systems actually work?**
* Can they **bridge product and customer environments?**

### AI Governance / Risk

* Do they understand **evaluation, controls, and responsible AI practices?**

If a bullet doesn’t support one of those — it’s wasted space.

---

# The biggest resume mistake senior candidates make

They write **task descriptions instead of capability evidence**.

Weak:

> Responsible for designing analytics solutions.

Strong:

> Designed enterprise analytics architectures integrating data pipelines, ML models, and BI platforms used by operational teams across finance and operations.

The difference is **credibility density**.

---

Recruiters rarely search tool lists for architect roles.

# Resume Length and Detail Options

There **are situations where a 1-page resume is more appropriate**, even for someone with extensive experience. But the key point is: **the reason is strategic, not seniority-based**. For someone with ~20+ years of experience like you, a **1-page resume is usually a specialized tool**, not your default resume.

Below are the main cases where a 1-page resume works well.

---

# When a 1-Page Resume Is Better

## 1. Networking or Referral Situations

When you’re sending a resume to someone who already knows you or has context.

Examples:

* A former colleague referring you
* A recruiter conversation
* Sending your background to a hiring manager after a call

Why 1 page works:

* The recipient already has context
* They just need **a quick credibility snapshot**

Typical format:

* Short summary
* Core capabilities
* Recent roles only

---

## 2. Early Screening / Recruiter Outreach

Sometimes recruiters ask for a resume before they really understand your background.

A **tight 1-pager** can help because:

* It removes overwhelm
* It emphasizes your **current positioning**
* It avoids “career archaeology”

This is especially useful when targeting roles like:

* Solutions Engineer
* Implementation Architect
* AI Governance Lead

where **clarity matters more than history**.

---

## 3. Role Pivoting

When you are repositioning yourself toward a **specific role category**.

For example:

If targeting **Solutions Engineer / Pre-Sales**, a 1-page resume can show:

* customer-facing architecture
* demos / PoCs
* enterprise AI / analytics
* technical credibility

while **removing unrelated earlier roles**.

This helps recruiters see the fit quickly.

---

## 4. Senior IC Roles (Not Management)

When the goal is to signal:

* **hands-on**
* **technical depth**
* **not an executive**

A long resume sometimes makes people assume:

* management focus
* high salary expectations
* strategic leadership role

A 1-page resume quietly signals:

> “I’m a focused senior individual contributor.”

---

# When 1 Page Is *Not* Ideal

For your situation, avoid 1 page when:

### Enterprise internal roles

Large companies often want to see **career progression**.

### Consulting firms

They want evidence of **client impact across multiple roles**.

### Governance / risk roles

These sometimes require showing **regulatory or industry context** over time.

In these cases **2 pages works better**.

---

# Avoid Resume Mistakes

For someone with your background — **senior, broad experience, AI/data/analytics, targeting IC architect roles** — there are a handful of resume mistakes that quietly reduce response rates. They rarely get discussed openly, but they matter because recruiters scan very quickly and make pattern judgments.

Below are the **most common mistakes experienced candidates make**, and how to fix them.

---

# 1. Leading With Career History Instead of Current Value

### The mistake

Opening the resume with a chronological narrative of the whole career.

Example:

> Over 25 years of experience in analytics, BI, data warehousing, and enterprise systems...

Why this hurts:

* It immediately signals **age and long tenure**
* It frames you as **career-long generalist**
* Recruiters want **current capability first**

### Better approach

Lead with **today’s positioning**.

Example:

> Senior solutions engineer and AI/analytics architect specializing in customer-facing architecture, enterprise AI platforms, and responsible AI deployment.

This tells the reader immediately:

* what you do
* where you fit
* why you’re relevant now

---

# 2. Too Much Detail From 20 Years Ago

### The mistake

Describing early-career roles with the same depth as recent roles.

Example:

```
Senior Data Architect (2003–2007)
• Designed data warehouse ETL pipelines using Informatica...
• Built reporting dashboards...
• Led BI migration...
```

Why this hurts:

* It suggests your **most relevant experience is old**
* It makes the resume feel like a **career archive**
* It wastes attention that should focus on recent work

### Better approach

Compress early roles.

Example:

```
Earlier Roles
Data Architect / Analytics Lead – Company
Designed enterprise analytics and reporting platforms.
```

Recruiters mainly care about **the last 10–12 years**.

---

# 3. Listing Technologies Instead of Capabilities

### The mistake

Huge tool lists.

Example:

```
Python, R, Spark, Hadoop, Kafka, Snowflake, Airflow,
dbt, Tableau, PowerBI, SQL, TensorFlow, PyTorch...
```

Why this hurts:

* Architect roles are not tool-driven
* Recruiters assume **hands-on coding role**
* It creates noise

### Better approach

Use **technology categories**.

Example:

```
AI / ML platforms
Analytics and data platforms
Data pipelines and integration
Cloud environments
LLM / generative AI architectures
```

Tools can appear **inside role bullets**, where they have context.

---

# 4. Describing Tasks Instead of Impact

### The mistake

Bullets describing responsibilities.

Example:

> Responsible for designing analytics solutions for enterprise clients.

Why this hurts:

* It sounds passive
* It lacks credibility
* It doesn’t differentiate you

### Better approach

Use **action → context → result**.

Example:

> Designed enterprise analytics architectures integrating data pipelines, ML models, and reporting platforms used by operations and finance teams.

This demonstrates:

* architecture thinking
* real-world application
* cross-functional value

---

# 5. Signaling Management When You Want IC Roles

This is a subtle but very common problem.

### The mistake

Language that suggests executive leadership.

Examples:

```
• Led large teams of engineers
• Responsible for departmental strategy
• Oversaw organization-wide transformation
```

Why this hurts:

* Recruiters assume **director-level candidate**
* They may think you are **overqualified**
* It conflicts with IC architect roles

### Better approach

Signal **technical leadership**, not organizational leadership.

Example:

```
• Led architecture design and technical decision-making for enterprise AI implementations
• Provided technical leadership across engineering, product, and customer teams
```

You are still senior — just **hands-on**.

---

# 6. Overly Long Resume

Another quiet bias trigger.

### The mistake

3–4 page resumes.

Why this hurts:

* Suggests **career historian**
* Hard to scan
* Increases “overqualified” perception

### Best practice

For your situation:

**2 pages maximum**

With:

* detailed recent roles
* compressed earlier roles

---

# 7. Hiding Customer-Facing Experience

For your target roles, this is critical.

Recruiters hiring for:

* Solutions Engineer
* Implementation Architect

want to see:

* customer interaction
* discovery
* demos
* architecture discussions

### The mistake

Describing only technical work.

### Better approach

Explicitly mention customer engagement.

Example:

```
• Led technical discovery sessions with enterprise customers translating business requirements into AI platform architectures.
```

This dramatically increases relevance for **pre-sales roles**.

---

# 8. Resume That Looks Like It Was Written for an ATS

Some resumes read like keyword stuffing.

Example:

```
AI
ML
Data
Cloud
Analytics
Architecture
```

Why this hurts:

* Humans reject them instantly

### Better approach

Use **natural sentences with embedded keywords**.

Example:

> Architected AI and analytics platforms integrating machine learning models, data pipelines, and enterprise reporting systems.

This works for both:

* ATS
* humans

# The real goal of a resume

Your resume isn’t a career record.

It’s a **pattern-matching document** that answers three questions quickly:

1. Does this person fit the role?
2. Do they have credible experience?
3. Are they likely to succeed here?

Everything in the resume should serve those questions.
